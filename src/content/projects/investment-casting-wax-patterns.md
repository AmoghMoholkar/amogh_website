Traditional investment casting relies on hand-tooled or injection-molded wax patterns, which is expensive and slow for one-off or low-volume parts. This project looked at substituting **additively manufactured patterns** — printed directly or used as masters — into a conventional shell-and-burnout investment casting workflow.

The core engineering question was practical: does the pattern-making method change downstream casting quality (dimensional accuracy, surface finish, defect rate), or is it a drop-in replacement once burnout parameters are tuned for the different material?

This project sits at the foundation of a broader interest in **additive manufacturing as a front-end to traditional metal-forming processes**, rather than as a replacement for them.
